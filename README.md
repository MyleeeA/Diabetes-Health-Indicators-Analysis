An Excel dashboard analyzing diabetes risk factors using CDC BRFSS 2015 data

## Project Overview
This project analyses 253,680 survey responses from the CDC's Behavioral Risk Factor 
Surveillance System (BRFSS) 2015, exploring the lifestyle, demographic, and health 
factors associated with diabetes diagnosis in the United States.

The dataset contains 22 variables including diabetes status, blood pressure, 
cholesterol, BMI, smoking, physical activity, income, and age, enabling a 
comprehensive view of diabetes risk across the population.

## Questions This Analysis Answers
- What percentage of the surveyed population has diabetes?
- Which health conditions are most common among diabetic patients?
- How does diabetes prevalence change across age groups?
- Does income level affect diabetes diagnosis rates?
- How do lifestyle factors like smoking and physical activity relate to diabetes?

## Key Findings
**Diabetes affects 13.9% of the surveyed population**
35,346 out of 253,680 respondents were diabetic or prediabetic, 
while 218,334 (86.1%) were not.

**High blood pressure is the strongest risk factor**
75.3% of diabetic patients had high blood pressure, compared to only 24.7% who did not, making it the most dominant risk factor identified in this dataset.

**High cholesterol affects 2 in 3 diabetic patients**
67.0% of diabetic respondents had high cholesterol, reinforcing the strong 
relationship between cardiovascular risk factors and diabetes.

**Diabetes risk rises steeply with age**
Cases climb dramatically from just 0.2% in the 18-24 age group, peaking at 
18.6% among 65-69 year olds, with the 60-69 age group carrying the 
highest share of all diabetes cases.

**Smoking is present in half of all diabetic cases**
51.8% of diabetic patients were smokers, suggesting a moderate but notable 
relationship between smoking and diabetes.

**Higher income groups show more diagnosed cases**
20.4% of diabetics earn over $75k vs only 6.7% earning under $10k, 
likely reflecting better healthcare access and diagnosis rates rather 
than wealth causing diabetes.
